An Unknown Opportunity ⮟

Born on 29th October 1935 in Ise (formerly Ujiyamada), Isao Takahata was not a proficient drawer, nor did he have a passion for animation. However, he still had a keen interest in the arts, particularly with music and literature. His childhood coincided with WWII and in 1945 he and his family survived a major United States air raid on Okayama City. The firebombing attack destroyed almost the entire city, including over 12,000 homes and killing more than 1,700 people.

In 1959, Takahata graduated from the University of Tokyo with a degree in French literature. It was during his studies when Takahata’s interest in animation began. He had seen the unfinished, but still released, French animated film The King and the Mockingbird (1952). Takahata became intrigued by the idea of using animation as a storytelling medium and decided he wished to write and direct animated films, rather than draw animations himself. Throughout his decades spanning career, Isao Takahata wrote and directed eight feature films, most of which were produced but the company he co-founded, Studio Ghibli.

Pre-Studio Ghibli Career ⮟

By chance, a friend suggested Takahata apply for a directing job at Toei Animation. After passing some entrance exams, he was hired by Toei Animation and began his professional career as an assistant director. He spent a number of years working on several of Toei’s films and television shows. Whilst working at Toei Animation he soon met another novice employee, aspiring animator and writer Hayao Miyazaki.

The Great Adventure of Horus, Prince of the Sun (1968) ⮟

Isao Takahata’s directorial debut came when asked by Toei Animation to direct their next film, The Great Adventure of Horus, Prince of the Sun (1968). Significantly, Hayao Miyazaki served as a key animator on this film. Although the film has gone on to be fairly well received, it was a total commercial failure and Takahata was demoted. Even still, the film marks the beginning of a long and fruitful career of professional collaborations and a lifelong friendship between Isao Takahata and Hayao Miyazaki.

Jarinko Chie (1981) ⮟

After spending the previous decade working on a variety of television projects and short films for a number of companies, Isao Takahata joined TMS Entertainment to make Jarinko Chan (1981); his first animated feature film as writer and director. Based on the manga of the same name, the film follows ten year old Chie who is tasked with helping her troublesome Yakuza father run a small tavern in Osaka. Following the film’s success, Takahata led production of a subsequent television spinoff.

Gauche the Cellist (1982) ⮟

Takahata’s next film, Gauche the Cellist (1982), was based on the children’s short story of the same name. Featuring the music of Ludwig van Beethoven, the film follows a young cellist who aspires to hone his craft while interacting with various wildlife that visit his house each night.

Studio Ghibli Career ⮟

Hayao Miyazaki and film producer Toshio Suzuki approached Isao Takahata with the idea of co-founding their own animation studio following the success of Miyazaki’s Nausicaä of the Valley of the Wind (1984). Subsequently, Studio Ghibli was formed on 15th June 1985 by founding members Isao Takahata, Hayao Miyazaki and film producers Toshio Suzuki and Yasuyoshi Tokuma. Both Takahata and Miyazaki have publically acknowledged the pivotal role of Suzuki in the establishment and success of Studio Ghibli.

“If it were not for Mr. Suzuki, there wouldn’t have been a Studio Ghibli” – Hayao Miyazaki

Primarily, Studio Ghibli would produce animated feature films directed by Hayao Miyazaki. Isao Takahata often served as a producer, as well as filling a variety of roles throughout the studio. Takahata would go on to become Studio Ghibli’s second most prolific director (behind Hayao Miyazaki) making five animated feature films, as well as one documentary, under the company’s name.

The Story of Yanagawa’s Canals (1987) ⮟

Isao Takahata’s first production for Studio Ghibli was in fact a live-action documentary; although the film does include some animated aspects. The documentary details the history of Yanagawa, Fukuoka, Japan and the locals’ struggle to preserve its famous canals from water pollution. Takahata initially travelled to Yanagawa to serve as a location scout for Miyazaki’s next film. However, after spending months exploring the city and waterways, as well as interacting with the local communities, Takahata decided to focus his efforts on producing a documentary.

Interestingly, as the film went over budget and years past the agreed deadline, Hayao Miyazaki began production on a new project to offset the additional costs of Takahata’s production. This grew into Laputa: Castle in the Sky (1986); a major financial success for Studio Ghibli in their early years.

“If Takahata had made his movie on schedule, Laputa wouldn’t have been born” – Toshio Suzuki

Grave of the Fireflies (1988) ⮟

Isao Takahata’s first animated feature film for Studio Ghibli was Grave of the Fireflies (1988); based on the semi-autobiographical short story of the same name. The film follows siblings Seita (fourteen years old) and Setsuko (four years old) and their struggle to survive the end of WWII in Japan. This is without doubt Studio Ghibli’s most mature work and Isao Takahata’s most important film. It is a harrowing and emotional depiction of the pernicious repercussions of war on a society; the civilians and most vulnerable therein. The film contains an early sequence depicting a visceral and devastating destruction of Kobe in a firebombing attack and it is difficult not to draw comparisons to Takahata’s real life experiences during the attack on Okayama City in his childhood.

The film has received universal critical acclaim and is often regarded as among the greatest and most powerful war films ever made. It is also notoriously considered one of the saddest films of all time.

A true cinematic masterpiece that must be seen by all.

Often labelled an anti-war film due to entirely focusing on the personal tragedies as a result of war, rather than to glamorise war as a heroic struggle between nations; a common criticism labelled at other so-called anti-war films. Grave of the Fireflies (1988) and My Neighbour Totoro (1988) were released theatrically in Japan as a dual bill. The stark contrast between the heart-warming, wondrous Totoro and the gut-wrenching, harrowing Fireflies has been described as “one of the most moving and remarkable double bills ever offered to a cinema audience”.

The juxtaposition between graphic, unfiltered effects of war and the unwavering joy and innocence of children, provides a poignant and emotional contrast in Grave of the Fireflies (1988). Paired with Michio Mamiya’s exceptional melancholic music score and the “almost minimalistic” animation style, invokes a truly heart-breaking atmosphere for the audience that is often difficult to watch; none more so than in the film’s happier moments, like watching two children catch fireflies.

Only Yesterday (1991) ⮟

Isao Takahata’s next film was Only Yesterday (1991), based on the manga of the same name. A rather unique film in the sense that it is a realistic, introspective drama aimed at mainly adult woman; a genre typically uncommon in animated feature films. The film follows Taeko, a twenty-seven year old unmarried office worker in Tokyo who decides to travel to the rural countryside to aid a safflower harvest over the summer; where she begins to recall memories of her childhood.

The film was a surprise box office success, attracting a large adult audience and becoming the highest-grossing Japanese film of 1991. Regularly described as “Studio Ghibli’s forgotten masterpiece” as it did not have a widespread North American release until 2016, but has still received universal critical acclaim.

Pom Poko (1994) ⮟

Takahata’s third animated feature for Ghibli was Pom Poko (1994). The film follows Tanuki (Japanese raccoon dogs), magical creatures capable of shape-shifting according to Japanese folklore, and their mischievous fun-loving antics. The film is essentially an environmental allegory, as the Tanuki fight back against a gigantic suburban development plan that threatens their forest habitat. The film was both a commercial and critical success, especially within Japan where the folklore-heavy and culturally relevant themes are easily recognisable.

My Neighbours the Yamadas (1999) ⮟

Arguably Studio Ghibli’s and Isao Takahata’s most experimental film, My Neighbours the Yamadas (1999) is a major stylistic departure from the traditional animation style presented in most of Ghibli’s other films. Rather than traditional hand-drawn cell animation, the film’s art style is more reminiscent of watercolour pictures. Additionally, instead of a typical start-to-finish narrative, the film is presented as a series of vignettes (short stories) following the daily lives of the Yamada family. Although not one of Ghibli’s most successful films, it received positive reviews for its unique visual style, its humour and observational look at modern Japanese suburban family life.

The Tale of the Princess Kaguya (2013) ⮟

After a fourteen year gap, Isao Takahata released his final film for Studio Ghibli. The Tale of the Princess Kaguya (2013) is based on a 10th century Japanese literary tale, The Tale of the Bamboo Cutter. The film follows a bamboo cutter who discovers a miniature baby girl inside a glowing bamboo shoot. Believing her to be a divine presence, the bamboo cutter and his wife decide to raise the child as their own. The mysterious young princess grows rapidly and enthrals all who encounter her, but ultimately she must confront her divine fate.

The film builds upon the stylistic foundations of Takahata’s previous film My Neighbour the Yamadas (1999), depicting an art style reminiscent of watercolour paintings.

Takahata felt this art style would help the audience emotionally connect with the film as they are able to “imagine the reality deep within the drawings”, rather than be distracted by a realistic art style. Feminism and the restriction of woman in traditional settings are core themes explored throughout the film, as well as the weight of expectations within social conformity and adulthood responsibility.

The Tale of the Princess Kaguya (2013) is among Studio Ghibli’s most acclaimed films and considered a modern animated classic. The film was nominated for both the Japan Academy Prize for Animation of the Year and the Academy Award for Best Animated Feature. The exquisite visual beauty, narrative depth and frank, melancholic honesty all combine to create an exceptional cinematic experience.

Legacy ⮟

Isao Takahata died on 5th April 2018, aged 82, following a battle with lung cancer. He enjoyed a long and successful career in the animation industry, undertaking a variety of roles within the production process. He is fondly remember as an icon of the industry and a crucial cog in the successes of one of the most famous animation studios ever created; the company he co-founded, Studio Ghibli. A film studio now synonymous with high-quality around the world. Although not a gifted animator himself, his initial intrigue in using animation as a storytelling medium proved to be a vindicating decision.

He wrote and directed some of the most exceptionally well crafted animated feature films ever made.

Most of his projects were uniquely mature character pieces aimed at adults, full of serious and unfiltered themes including the cost of war, environmentalism and adulthood responsibility. Particular mention must go to the gorgeous and experimental The Tale of the Princess Kaguya (2013) and the timeless, yet harrowing Grave of the Fireflies (1988).

A farewell ceremony was held at the Ghibli Museum in Tokyo, where Hayao Miyazaki spoke publically about Takahata for the first time following his passing:
“I was convinced that Paku-san [Takahata’s nickname] would live to be 95 years old, but unfortunately he passed away. It makes me think my time is also limited… Thank you, Paku-san.” – An emotional Hayao Miyazaki.

Perhaps unjustly, he is less of a household name than lifelong friend Hayao Miyazaki and his films only achieved modest commercial success in comparison. But what is certain is that Studio Ghibli, and the animation industry as a whole, would not be the same today without Isao Takahata’s immeasurable contributions.
