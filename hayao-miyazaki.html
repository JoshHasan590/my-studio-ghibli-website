A Love of Drawing ⮟

Born on 5th January 1941 in Tokyo, Hayao Miyazaki had always demonstrated a passion for manga and animation from a young age. During his school years he aspired to become a manga artist and much of his early drawings were of planes, tanks and battleships as he initially struggled to draw people. He destroyed most of these as he felt copying other artist’s styles was preventing his own development as an artist. Later he would graduate from Gakushuin University with degrees in political science and economics. During his studies, he would regularly visit his middle-school art teacher; sketching and drawing manga in his studio.

His interest in animation was sparked by the film Panda and the Magic Serpent (1958); Japan’s first feature-length animated film in colour. He later recounted that the film left a profound impression on him and that he was “moved to the depth of his soul”. Throughout his decades spanning career, Hayao Miyazaki has written and directed eleven feature films, most of which were produced by the company he co-founded, Studio Ghibli.

Pre-Studio Ghibli Career ⮟

He began his professional career in 1963 at Toei Animation, working as an in-between artist. This involved drawing the intermediate frames between two key frames, a process used to construct the illusion of movement in classic hand-drawn animation. Within 12 months of his arrival he became the chief secretary of Toei Animation’s labour union and strove to improve the treatment of animators; an issue still present within the industry today.

Whilst working at Toei Animation he soon met another novice employee, aspiring director Isao Takahata. During his time at Toei Animation, Miyazaki grew to work as a key animator on a number of projects, most notably on The Great Adventure of Horus, Prince of the Sun (1968); significantly Takahata’s feature length directorial debut. This marks the beginning of long and fruitful career of professional collaborations and a lifelong personal friendship between the two.

The Castle of Cagliostro (1979) ⮟

Miyazaki’s breakthrough moment came with his directorial debut, The Castle of Cagliostro (1979); a Lupin III film. Serving as writer, director and key animator, the film is truly of Miyazaki’s making and it went on to become the most successful entry in the Lupin III franchise. The changes made to the characters behaviours and personality traits were initially not well received by fans of the pre-existing material. However, the decision to move away from the tired and crude caricatures, towards more family friendly and well-rounded character work is, somewhat ironically, considered the moment Lupin “grew up”. The Castle of Cagliostro is a massively influential film; now seen as an early anime classic. The detail in animation, particularly the environments and backgrounds, was unrivalled at the time. The Castle of Cagliostro provides an identifiable benchmark and foundation of the signature visual, story and thematic styles, which would come to define Hayao Miyazaki’s career.

Nausicaä of the Valley of the Wind (1984) ⮟

In the years following The Castle of Cagliostro’s release, Hayao Miyazaki began writing and illustrating a manga series titled Nausicaä of the Valley of the Wind, with the first chapter releasing in 1982. Following the success of his manga, Miyazaki agreed to a film adaption, on the condition that he would serve as writer, director and animator. Nausicaä of the Valley of the Wind would release in 1984. Although it released prior to Studio Ghibli’s creation, it is retroactively considered a Ghibli film due to, not only Miyazaki’s, but all of the Ghibli founders’ involvement in its production.

Set in a post-post-apocalyptic world inhabited by giant mutant insects and a vast poisonous jungle, Nausicaä, the princess of the Valley of the Wind, aims to bring peace between waring nations amid the toxic jungle’s constant expansion. The film released to universal critical acclaim and is now often named among the best anime feature films ever made; regular occurrences throughout Miyazaki’s career. Specific praise is often reserved for the script, soundtrack and the titular female lead character, Nausicaä.

In 1985, a heavily edited English-dubbed adaption titled Warriors of the Wind was released in the United States. This was a commercial and critical disaster. The changes and edits impact the plot and heavily diluted the narrative themes of environmentalism and the cost of war. Ridiculed by Miyazaki, and as a result, many of his and Studio Ghibli’s films would remain “un-dubbed” and not widely released internationally until the mid-1990s with Disney’s collaborations.

Studio Ghibli Career ⮟

Studio Ghibli was formed on 15th June 1985 by founding members Hayao Miyazaki, Isao Takahata and film producers Toshio Suzuki and Yasuyoshi Tokuma. Both Miyazaki and Takahata have publically acknowledged the pivotal role of Suzuki in the establishment and success of Studio Ghibli.

“If it were not for Mr. Suzuki, there wouldn’t have been a Studio Ghibli” – Hayao Miyazaki

Being a founding member and key figure at the company, Miyazaki would often collaborate on Studio Ghibli productions that were not his own; regularly serving as an executive producer, animator or even as a screenwriter.

Laputa: Castle in the Sky (1986) ⮟

One year on, the first official Studio Ghibli production was released. Hayao Miyazaki’s third film Laputa: Castle in the Sky (1986). Crucially for Ghibli in their infancy, the film released to major commercial success, becoming Japan’s highest-grossing animated film of the year. The film’s aesthetic is credited as a significant milestone in popularising both the steampunk and dieselpunk sci-fi sub-genres.

My Neighbour Totoro (1988) ⮟

Miyazaki’s next film was My Neighbour Totoro (1988). The production was rather chaotic, particularly for the artists, as the film was simultaneously produced alongside Isao Takahata’s Grave of the Fireflies (1988). The film simply follows a father and his two young daughters, Satsuki and Mei, approximately ten and four years old, moving to an old rural house and their adventures with the wondrous forest spirits that live nearby; the titular Totoros.

It received widespread critical acclaim and garnered a cult following due to its relatively low box office return. In the years following its release, the film has become something of a cultural phenomenon. The titular character Totoro is among the most recognisable in all of anime, as well as becoming the official mascot and logo for Studio Ghibli. It is estimated to have earned over $300m in box office and home video sales to date, and over $1bn in officially licensed merchandise.

My Neighbour Totoro (1988) and Grave of the Fireflies (1988) were released theatrically in Japan as a dual bill. The stark contrast between the heart-warming, wondrous Totoro and the gut-wrenching, harrowing Fireflies has been described as “one of the most moving and remarkable double bills ever offered to a cinema audience”.

Kiki’s Delivery Service (1989) ⮟

To finish the 1980s Hayao Miyazaki released Kiki’s Delivery Service (1989). An adaption of a popular novel, the film is about a thirteen year old witch, Kiki, who moves to a new town and tries to earn a living through her ability to fly. It is a classic coming-of-age story following our protagonist’s vulnerability and subsequent maturity. The film was a massive commercial success becoming the highest-grossing film in Japan in 1989.

Porco Rosso (1992) ⮟

Hayao Miyazaki began the 1990s with his release of Porco Rosso (1992). Miyazaki’s love of aviation is on full display as the film follows a former WWI fighter pilot now working as a freelance bounty hunter catching “air pirates”. However, a curse has transformed him into an anthropomorphic pig. The film is one of the more mature in Miyazaki’s catalogue, but this did not impact its success as it remained the highest-grossing animated film in Japan for several years.

Princess Mononoke (1997) ⮟

After spending most of the mid-1990s in production, Princess Mononoke was released in 1997. Set in the late Muromachi period of Japan (approx. 1336 – 1573 CE) the film follows a young prince searching for a cure to save his life from a deadly curse. He is inadvertently entangled in a war between the Gods of the forests and the mining colony destroying it. One of Studio Ghibli’s most mature works, the film’s central theme is environmentalism and humanity’s relationship with the natural world. Princess Mononoke (1997) is considered a landmark film in the world of animation due to its breath-taking visuals, epic story and morally ambiguous, well-written characters. In particular, the representation of woman and people with disabilities have been singled out for praise. The film was a commercial and critical blockbuster, becoming Japan’s highest-grossing film of the year and breaking the box office record for a domestic film.

It is the first animated feature film to win the Japan Academy Prize for Best Picture.

Additionally, the film is credited with greatly increasing Studio Ghibli’s popularity and influence worldwide. Miramax acquired the North American distribution rights for the film. As a result of the heavily edited Warriors of the Wind version of Nausicaä, Studio Ghibli had enforced a strict no edits policy. When a suggestion was made to edit Princess Mononoke (1997), it is rumoured that Studio Ghibli responded by sending Miramax an authentic Japanese katana with the message: “No Cuts.”

Spirited Away (2001) ⮟

Miyazaki himself had suggested Princess Mononoke (1997) would be his final film. However, it was not. The story goes that while holidaying with family friends, which included multiple young girls, he realised he had not created a film for ten year old girls. Thus, he set out to do so. Most Japanese entertainment produced for that demographic at the time revolved around childhood and teenage romance. Miyazaki felt that was not true or relatable to girls of that age and wanted to create a female heroine that they could look up too. This became Chihiro of Spirited Away (2001).

Spirited Away, considered by many to be Hayao Miyazaki’s magnum opus, was released in 2001 and has become a worldwide phenomenon. Chihiro and her parents are moving to a small town in the Japanese countryside and while exploring, accidently enter the spirit world. Her parents are turned into pigs by an evil witch and it is up to Chihiro to save them and return to their world, before she forgets who she is.

A masterpiece in every sense of the word, Spirited Away (2001) is simply a must watch film. It became the highest-grossing film in Japanese history, a record held for nineteen years, until 2020. It is often an international viewer’s introduction into Studio Ghibli and anime as a whole. The film has won countless awards and accolades worldwide, including the Japan Academy Prize for Best Film. Its international recognition was sealed where it won the Academy Award for Best Animated Feature at the 75th Academy Awards, making the first (and only to date) hand-drawn, non-English language film to do so. Hayao Miyazaki did not attend the awards ceremony in the United States. Years later Miyazaki revealed he didn’t attend the ceremony as a silent protest against the United States invasion of Iraq.

“The reason I wasn’t here for the Academy Award was because I didn’t want to visit a country that was bombing Iraq. At the time, my producer shut me up and did not allow me to say that, but I don’t see him around today. By the way, my producer also shared in that feeling.” – Hayao Miyazaki

Howl’s Moving Castle (2004) ⮟

Hayao Miyazaki’s anti-war stance was present in his next feature, Howl’s Moving Castle (2004), loosely based on the novel of the same name. Set in a fictional kingdom with the prevalence of magic, and against the backdrop of a war with another kingdom, Sophie, a young milliner, is turned into an elderly woman by a witch. After encountering a wizard named Howl, she is caught up in his resistance to fighting for the king. The most recognisable element of the film is certainly the moving castle. Heavily inspired by the steampunk genre, the astonishing detail and unique movement is one of Miyazaki’s finest designs. It has become one of the most commercially successful films in Japanese history, grossing over $200m worldwide. It was also nominated for the Academy Award for Best Animated Feature.

Ponyo (2008) ⮟

Toshio Suzuki suggested to Miyazaki to aim his next film at children. After spending time at a seaside town and inspired by Hans Christian Anderson’s The Little Mermaid, Miyazaki’s created Ponyo (2008). The film tells the story of Ponyo, a goldfish who escapes the ocean and is rescued by a five year old boy, and her desire to become a human girl. Intending to be a celebration of childhood innocence and cheerfulness, Ponyo (2008) was met with, the now routine for Miyazaki, commercial success and critical acclaim. During production Miyazaki decided he wanted to use only traditional animation. He was heavily involved in the hand-drawn animation and the film features 170,000 frames, a record for Miyazaki.

The Wind Rises (2013) ⮟

Hayao Miyazaki’s love for aviation has always been identifiable in his works. It is most prevalent in The Wind Rises (2013). It is a fictionalised biographical film of Jiro Horikoshi (1903 – 1982), a designer of fighter aircraft used by the Empire of Japan in WWII.

Miyazaki was inspired after reading a quote of Horikoshi’s: “All I wanted to do was to make something beautiful."

It was intended to release simultaneously with Isao Takahata’s The Tale of the Princess Kaguya (2013), but a delay in Takahata’s production meant this did not come to fruition. Yet another critical and commercial success for Miyazaki, the film won the Japan Academy Prize for both Animation of the Year and Best Musical Score, as well as another Academy Award nomination for Best Animated Feature. Less than two months after its theatrical release, Hayao Miyazaki announced his retirement from the production of feature films at the age of 72. The Wind Rises (2013) has been described as a fittingly bittersweet swansong for director Hayao Miyazaki.

The Red Turtle (2016) ⮟

In 2016 Studio Ghibli released their first non-Japanese film, a co-production with French company Wild Bunch, titled The Red Turtle (2016). Written and directed by Dutch animator Michaël Dudok de Wit, the film tells the story of a man shipwrecked on a deserted island, where he encounters a giant red sea turtle.

The film has no dialogue.

Reportedly, years prior Hayao Miyazaki had seen Dudok de Wit’s Academy Award winning short animated film Father and Daughter (2000). Miyazaki personally contacted Dudok de Wit with the offer to produce a feature film for him. Becoming one of Studio Ghibli’s most critically acclaimed works and earning an Academy Award nomination for Best Animated Feature, The Red Turtle (2016) is one of their most unique films. Unfortunately, it did not break into the animation mainstream, but nevertheless gained itself a successful cult following. A project that likely would not exist without Hayao Miyazaki connecting with an aspiring animator and director, Michaël Dudok de Wit.

Future & Legacy ⮟

How Do You Live? (TBA) ⮟

In 2017 it was announced that Hayao Miyazaki would come out of retirement to make another feature film, titled How Do You Live? In the years since, little has been made public about the film. It is known that the film is based on a 1937 novel of the same name, however much like Miyazaki’s previous adaptions, the film will likely have significant differences. As expected, it is being hand-drawn by Miyazaki and in 2021 it was announced that approximately half the animation was complete. There is currently no attached release date and although Hayao Miyazaki and producer Toshio Suzuki remain tight-lipped regarding the film’s production, there is a growing belief that fans of Miyazaki will not have to wait too much longer.

A Master of His Craft ⮟

Hayao Miyazaki has enjoyed a long and successful career at the very top of his profession. He is regularly compared to Walt Disney for his role in establishing such a recognisable company in Studio Ghibli. A film studio now synonymous with high-quality around the world. His films can be characterised by the recurrence of themes such as pacifism, environmentalism, love and friendship to name but a few. The themes and meanings behind his films share and reflect his own ideals, which have no doubt influenced his audiences. His narratives are notable for their encapsulating atmospheres, thought-provoking stories and complex characters. In particular, his progressive and positive portrayal of female characters, who contain complex and individual characteristics, has led many to describe him as a feminist and his heroines as role models.

Professional recognition followed Miyazaki throughout his career, leading to his films amassing over 100 separate awards, as well as regularly setting and breaking various worldwide and domestic records. Highlights include multiple Japan Academy Prizes and an Academy Award for Spirited Away (2001) in the Best Animated Feature category. Additionally, he was awarded the Academy Honorary Award in 2014 for his impact and contributions to animation and cinema as a whole. In 2012, he was awarded the honour of Person of Cultural Merit by the Japanese government, for outstanding cultural contributions via his films.

Hayao Miyazaki is often described as the godfather of anime.

His films shaped the future of not only animation, but filmmaking in general. A proponent of classic, hand-drawn animation, he and his films continue to inspire countless animators, directors and writers around the world. The jaw dropping visuals, across a vast array of undeniably fantastic films, cements his legacy as quite simply, one of the greatest filmmakers of all time.
